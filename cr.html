Critical Reflection

In this part, I am focusing on sorting out my practice process, ideas and changes in Unit1

My dreams often feel very real to me. Through those disturbing images in my mind, I feel that dreams are an extension my emotions. Even though the images aren’t real, they can keep my mind in a state of intense emotions.

The project started with thinking about the connection between dreams and simulacra.When a person is dreaming, the brain will perceive simple images in the form of a dream, but the brain can’t use complex functions. The transmitting and receiving nerves are also mostly shut-off and can’t process time or space, much less recognize the realness of the images. The imagery in people’s dreams are similar to simulacra. In a way, trying to forget the deception of dreams is the pursuit of reality.

I’m the creator of my dreams, yet I try to forget the deceptions of my dreams after I wake up. This contradiction reflects my fear of losing the sense of self in a way. The smoke generated from firing ceramics dissipates in a cycle, similar to fleeting moments of dreams that I grasp onto and then force myself to forget about. People are often afraid of being deceived, yet our minds deceive us. We are afraid of losing our sense of self, but we create this feeling of deception in our own subconscious. I tried to capture this state of mind in my work.

a Simulacra

“The ability of an image to distort the real. An image is an 'apparent falsification' of the real; it is considered to be an illusion.”

Jean Baudrillard, Simulations (1983)
While working on this piece, I continued reading Baudrillard's "Simulacra and Simulation" and researching about simulacra, which deepened my understanding of this term. In terms of imitation, simulacrum refers to the realization of something that is uncommunicable and irreproducible. The term originates from the Latin word simulare, which means to copy, represent, or feign.

In the late Roman Empire, it referred to the statues at the entrance of cities. Later, the term was extended to represent images, language, and written forms. Mimesis is the transposition of illusions and can be considered as (or equivalent to) artificial artifacts of illusions. Mimesis is not a poor imitation of visible things, but an artificial simulation of unrepresentable illusions.

We will find that everything comes from nature. In this open world, all of nature becomes our material. We choose trees, leaves, wood, and stones, forming a certain relationship with nature. Our understanding of self will gradually change. I am considering what kind of change it is.

For example, is the tree I see really a tree? In fact, most of the time, the tree is no longer what I see with my naked eye. These things come from computers and the screens of my mobile phone. I see green plants on the mobile screen, but it is not natural, it is not what I see with my naked eye. All of these are called simulacra.

Simulacra disrupt the most fundamental and primitive association between humans and nature. When I truly see plants with my naked eye, there will be wind, breath, and scent, rather than a cold digital image telling me that it is a tree. I analyze it as a tree because my past visual experiences tell me that, "Oh, this should be a tree!" But in reality, at that moment, it is not a tree. In fact, what I see is still a simulacrum.

Dreams, at a certain moment, when your eyes are closed, all the imagined shapes and images are similar to organic simulacra in the human brain. Therefore, I am contemplating that if we follow this logic, dreams have a certain degree of deception.

b Clay and the Subconscious

The sculpture "TRACE" by Sui Jianguo has greatly inspired me in terms of material selection. Through the direct and profound tactile contact of his hands with clay, he creates sculptures by molding, squeezing, separating, and twisting, which allows him to contemplate the intrinsic connection between the artist's essence and the sculpture itself. This has made me ponder the essence of clay. Clay is a natural material that comes from nature.It has a very unique nature: it has tension and perception. When people apply force to the clay, it’s like a mutual perception between people and nature, creating a dialogue between physical objects and mental images. I “create” things through understanding of nature and my perception at the moment, giving form to my imagination.  

c Blank Space

1. Lee Ufan
Lee Ufan promotes another idea of ‘What you see is what you don’t see’. His works invite us to pay attention to the empty space, to discover and feel the tension generated by the untouched areas of the canvas. 

2.GuoXi
Guo Xi, a painter from Song Dynasty, suggested in “Mountains have Three Distances” that perception is a distillation of the mind's intermingling with the realm through the practical observation of the landscape. He suggested that the landscape could remove mundane emotions, even though the landscape itself is just a material object. In order to break this barrier, Guo Xi proposes the concept of “farness” to extend the shape of the landscape. The viewer's eye is inevitably shifted to his imagination, thus underlining the “nothingness” in the distance.It can also be found through research that the space of Taihu stone and the space of Chinese painting have a very large similarity.
