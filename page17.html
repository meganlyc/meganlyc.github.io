Research Festival

For the Research Festival, I will continue my exploration of food materials in design and architectural applications through a unique material-based expression. Building on my experiment with tea bricks as sculptures in the Summer Show, this project has inspired me to delve deeper into the potential of food materials—not only as decorative elements but also for their structural and functional value.

In this project, I plan to observe explorations by others in the fields of architecture and fashion, drawing inspiration to speculate on the potential future applications of these materials in design. I will analyze the performance of food-based materials in architecture, environmental design, and fashion, while conducting experimental explorations to imagine how these materials could address ecological challenges. For instance, I envision repurposing food materials to create fabrics for fashion products or using them to construct futuristic sculptures, architectural structures, and even imagined island cities. This exploration seeks to uncover innovative, multidisciplinary applications of food-based materials.

The outcomes of this research will be presented in the form of a handmade book and models. The handmade book will showcase the textures of food-based materials as fabrics for fashion product design, while the models will focus on their applications in architecture. Through these presentations, I aim to inspire the audience to consider the possibilities of food-based materials in sustainable design and their potential for interdisciplinary innovation in the future.

Handmade Book Section:

Through my research, I have discovered that apple peels are used to make shoes, coconut shells are used to make concrete, and corn is processed into fibers. However, many of these involve extracting substances from the food. I am now considering: what if the material retains a high content of the original food? How would that work in design and construction?

I know that CSM has a materials store, so I went to take a look and found many samples made from food-based alternatives.

I attempted to create biomaterials myself to observe the texture and condition of materials when food makes up a significant proportion of their composition.

I used leftover or expired everyday food, combined with glycerin and substances such as sodium alginate, agar, carrageenan, or starch (e.g., corn starch), to experiment with creating various materials and fabrics with different levels of hardness and textures.


My initial idea is to create a flag book,  with different materials attached to each small page. When the book stands upright, it gives the impression of an architectural structure. However, I’ve noticed that some of the material samples I’ve made tend to warp or bend, while others are extremely fragile. I’m not sure if this presentation method is the best option. I will need to experiment further.

Additionally, I want to create a three-dimensional model that explores the use of food in future architecture.

I am considering some visual concepts for presentation:

1. Using Tetris as a reference to create a Tetris-like structure made of food, situating this building by the seaside.

Here are the key reasons behind this idea:
1. From “Future” to “Technology” and “Gaming”

When thinking about the “future,” my first association is technology. The future is often linked to advanced technologies, innovative materials, and even concepts we cannot yet imagine. Technology makes everything more flexible and full of possibilities. Similarly, the idea of gaming comes to mind when thinking about the future because games represent a kind of limitless creativity. In the gaming world, everything can change freely—buildings can float, move, or defy the rules of reality. This aligns perfectly with the imagination of the future.
2. Why Tetris?

Thinking of gaming, I quickly associated it with Tetris. The game’s unique modular, puzzle-like assembly method stands out. Each block can exist independently or combine into various forms. This inspired the idea: could future architecture be just as flexible? Instead of fixed buildings, could modular structures adapt their shapes as needed, even move? Such architecture would not only be flexible but also sustainable. For instance, repairs or adjustments might only require replacing or modifying specific modules rather than demolishing the entire structure.
3. Considering the environment where architecture exists (exploring material potential): perhaps the seaside?

During a tutorial, I learned about a habitat construction case that used biomaterials like pineapple fibers and coconut shells to create small ecosystems along canals or rivers, restoring environments and providing habitats for flora and fauna. This inspired me to consider the role and functionality of architecture in its environment.

The seaside is an incredibly fragile ecosystem, facing threats like rising sea levels and coastal erosion. This led to the idea: what if architecture adopted a “Tetris-like” modular design, capable of adjusting its structure in response to imminent natural disasters? For instance, during a tsunami or storm surge, the modules could be reconfigured to form a breakwater or flood barrier, protecting inland areas from seawater intrusion.

Moreover, if these modules were made of food-based materials, they would not only be eco-friendly but also provide some disaster-resistance capabilities. After use, these materials could naturally degrade and integrate into the environment, acting as “nourishment” for the ecosystem and reducing pollution. In this way, architecture would not only serve as a living space but also become part of the environment—adaptively responding to natural challenges and achieving harmonious coexistence with the ecosystem.
