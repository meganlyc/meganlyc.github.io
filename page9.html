Yoko Ono's work inTate Art Museum

Yoko Ono’s art prompted me to deeply consider the relationship between sculpture and photography.

Walking through the gallery showcasing Yoko Ono’s works, I was struck by her innovative approach to combining physical installations with their photographic representations. In the exhibition, Ono’s pieces such as “Waterdrop Painting”and A Work To Be Stepped On”exemplify this duality. We can see in the image “Waterdrop Painting” a hanging jar filled with water, presented both as a tangible object and as a photograph. This dual presentation challenges viewers to reconsider the transient nature of art and the interaction between different mediums.

Another part of the gallery features, where fabric is laid on the floor with a label reading “A work to be stepped on.” The physical interaction of stepping on the artwork adds an experiential layer, which is then immortalized through photography. This piece blurs the lines between sculpture and photography, transforming the viewer’s perception and engagement with the artwork.

In my practice, I strive to evoke a similar sense of transformation. The work which I created, combines ceramics, paper pulp, and fabric into a whimsical, fantastical form. The playful elements, like the black cat and bright red teeth, contrast with Ono’s minimalism but share a common thread of reimagining everyday materials.

However, displaying my sculpture online shifts the focus to its commercial aspect. Unlike the tactile experience of Ono’s installations,I let my piece becomes a commodity, interacting with viewers through a digital screen. This transformation raises questions about the value and interaction of art when mediated by technology. The sculpture, now a photograph, loses some of its physical presence but gains accessibility .

While Ono’s pieces are deeply rooted in conceptual engagement, my work navigates the intersection of physical and digital spaces. This exploration helps me understand how different mediums can coexist and complement each other, enriching the artistic experience.

TIM LEWIS - 'The Forest Visits'

Tim Lewis' sculptures seamlessly blend surrealist and mechanical elements to create a mixture that is both mysterious and alive, and I am amazed. His work pushes me to contemplate the boundaries between life forms and objects and how these boundaries can be redefined through art.

Lewis's pieces are immersive, interactive experiences that invite the audience to engage with a montage of theatrics. By employing electronic programming and physics, he brings to life these intricate and whimsical creatures, aiming to raise awareness of endangered animals that share our planet and are on the brink of disappearing. This approach prompts us to reflect on our impact on the environment in a deeply emotional and thought-provoking way.

Lewis integrates everyday objects with fantastical elements, creating a juxtaposition that challenges traditional notions of sculpture. For instance, the hanging deer head with a guitar body seems to symbolize the intersection of nature and culture. Similarly, the use of scissors and butterflies juxtaposes vulnerability with function, evoking a sense of balance and potential destruction. I'm very interested in this similar theme of mixing and transforming. My work incorporates whimsical and fantastical elements, reflecting my interest in how disparate parts can come together to create a cohesive whole. This approach situates my research within a broader discourse on the fluidity of forms and the interplay between natural and artificial constructs.


Reflecting on the essence of art as the form of reality at a specific moment in time, Lewis's works capture the zeitgeist of our environmental concerns. Art has become a powerful medium for documenting and responding to the critical issues of our era. By freezing these moments and concerns in his sculptures, Lewis not only highlights the current state of the world but also provokes contemplation and dialogue about our collective future.

Sin Wai Kin - 'It's Always You'

After seeing Sin Wai Kin's work, I am struck by how she uses costume and performance to challenge and deconstruct gender norms. Her work drag performances, and infused with speculative fiction, reveal the fluidity and performative nature of identity. This perspective has significantly influenced my approach to sculpture, where I combine whimsical and fantastical elements and try to create hybrid forms that blur the lines between human, animal, and machine.

This reflection naturally remind me of the Bauhaus movement, particularly its exploration of costume and performance. The Bauhaus emphasis on integrating art, craft, and technology aligns with my practice. The geometric and abstract forms of Bauhaus costumes, especially in Oskar Schlemmer’s "Triadic Ballet," inspire the structural and material choices in my sculptures. The way Bauhaus artists transformed performers into living sculptures through costume design deeply informs my creative process.

Both Sin Wai Kin and Bauhaus highlight the transformative power of costume in art. And in a way, it talks about the nature and interconnectedness of the natural world and the artificial world. These influences also inspire me to think about the next phase of my work-considering how I might animate this sculpture. Utilizing holographic projection to make it move would create a dynamic, immersive experience that resonates with the idea of transformation and hybridity. I realize I have to continue this exploration.
