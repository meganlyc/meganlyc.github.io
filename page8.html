The First layer（ceramic）

1：Hand Shaping
The process begins with me hand-molding the clay, using only my hands and simple tools to shape each piece. 

2. First Firing
After the piece is shaped, it undergoes its first firing in the kiln at 1230 degrees Celsius. This high temperature firing, known as bisque firing, hardens the clay and prepares it for glazing. This step is vital as it turns the delicate raw clay into a sturdy, porous structure.

3. Sanding and Glazing
Once the bisque firing is complete, I carefully sand the piece to smooth out any imperfections and ensure a perfect surface for glazing. I then apply a carefully selected glaze that will enhance the texture and color of the piece. This stage requires a meticulous hand and a keen eye for detail to ensure the glaze adheres well and displays the desired effects after the final firing.

4. Final Firing
The last step is another round in the kiln, where the glaze melts and forms a glass-like coating that is both beautiful and functional. This final firing solidifies the glaze, melding it with the bisque-fired body to produce the glossy, finished product you see in the last image.

The Second layer（papier-mâché）

I sculpt using vibrant red paper pulp, a crucial stage that adds both structure and striking visual interest to my creations. 


The Third layer（acrylic）

I use a laser cutter to precisely fabricate acrylic rings and discs. After cutting these shapes, I drill holes through them, which allows me to assemble the structure. I then secure four acrylic discs together using acrylic rods, ensuring stability and uniformity in my designs.

Modeling

The Fourth layer（white resin，spunbond fabric）

In this part , I utilize white resin for 3D printing to form the base structure of my artwork. After the 3D printing is complete, I meticulously wrap the structure with a material that resembles spunbond fabric, yet possesses the sturdiness of stiff gauze. This unique combination not only reinforces the sculpture but also adds a delicate, textile-like texture that contrasts intriguingly with the solid form of the resin base. This layering technique is pivotal in achieving the depth and complexity in my designs, reflecting my ongoing exploration of material properties and artistic expression.

The Fifth layer（plaster，plastiline clay, demin, buttion)

In this part of my artistic process, I focus on creating a plaster sculpture of a cat's head and a distinctively crafted mask.

1.Mold Making and and Plaster Pouring
I start by using diatomaceous earth to make a precise mold, capturing the intricate details necessary for the plaster base of the cat's head.Once the mold is set, I pour plaster into it to form the initial sculpture model. This stage is crucial for establishing the solid structure of the cat's head.

2.Mask Shaping:
For the mask, I employ plastiline clay, which I shape and mold using warm water. The mask is worked until it hardens and loses its transparency, indicating readiness for painting and decoration.

3.Decoration:
I paint the hardened mask with acrylics, adding vibrant colors and life to the piece. Additionally, I embellish the mask with contrasting textures: plant motifs on the left side to create visual tension and expansion, and denim fabric with buttons on the right side, adding a tactile diversity and an element of everyday charm.

The Other layer

In this segment of the process, I found myself contemplating the structural foundation of my sculpture. I initially planned to use a triangular frame as the base. To explore this idea, I experimented with colored paper pulp to create a vibrant yellow component resembling a piece of cheese. This piece serves a dual purpose: it is both an integral part of the sculpture and an experimental element that may or may not be included in the final design. This phase reflects my ongoing exploration and flexibility in sculpture, allowing each piece to evolve organically through creative experimentation.
