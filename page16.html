Supporting work and future plan

Supporting work: food container

The creation of a ding vessel using tea, a consumable material, invites contemplation on the paradox of function and material.
Traditionally, a ding is a vessel meant to contain and preserve, often symbolizing abundance, sustenance, and ritual significance.
By constructing it from tea -an item typically consumed rather than preserved -the work challenges the conventional roles of both container and content, leading viewers to question the boundaries between utility and symbolism.
This tea ding becomes a meditation on the cycles of consumption and containment. In this form, food transitions from nourishment to structure, from something transient to something enduring. It raises the question: can food, when repurposed as art, still retain its inherent connection to sustenance, or does it transform into something entirely different? This duality allows the ding to exist as both a celebration of food's cultural significance and a playful exploration of its material potential beyond consumption.
Furthermore, this work prompts reflection on the cultural and philosophical implications of using food to create a food container. It underscores the layered relationships humans have with what they consume, challenging us to see food not only as sustenance but also as a medium for memory, ritual, and artistic expression. In merging container with content, the tea ding emphasizes the unity of function and material, inviting viewers to rethink the fluidity between what we eat and what sustains.

Thinking about the future development plan:

1.Beyond Architecture: Exploring Tea Bricks or Food as a Monument

In my future creative plans, beyond their application in architecture, I am also considering using tea bricks, tea leaves, or other food-based materials to create monumental sculptures as a new avenue of exploration. When viewers first encounter my sculptures in an exhibition, they often fail to recognize that the material is tea bricks, mistaking it for wood or other common industrial materials. This misperception has led me to think about the association between tea bricks and industrial materials, as well as their connection to the monumental form.

The tea bricks I press and use are weighty cubes with modular geometric shapes that naturally imbue them with a sense of solidity and permanence. Because of these qualities, tea bricks may have the potential to become public sculptures. Placed outdoors, their density and mass evoke the gravitas of traditional monuments. However, unlike the permanence of traditional monuments, tea bricks, as a food-based material, are susceptible to the elements, dissolving over time as they interact with wind and rain.

This transformation presents a unique opportunity for exploration—introducing the impact of time and the environment into a monumental sculpture, creating an “ephemeral monument” effect. Such a monument would challenge the traditional concept of permanence in commemorative sculptures. By allowing the material to dissolve and change over time, it provides viewers with a unique perspective, turning the monument into a dynamic object that interacts with its surroundings and evolves gradually. This approach challenges the conventional monument’s reliance on and expectation of “eternity,” offering instead a reinterpretation of what it means to commemorate and endure.


2. On Tea Stain Creations (Painting)

After the exhibition, I flipped over the tea leaf strips used in the display and unexpectedly discovered that time had left behind tea stains on the paper, creating a unique “tea stain” effect. These imprints resemble natural textures on the paper, carrying an uncontrollable, organically generated beauty. They not only document the interaction between the material and the paper but also reflect the traces of change brought about by the passage of time. This discovery led me to wonder: could these tea stains be further developed into a part of my painting practice, capturing moments shaped by the interplay of time and materials?

This form of tea stain painting offers a new perspective for creation—not merely “drawing” patterns but “generating” them through the natural transformation of the material itself. This generative process has prompted me to consider the dialectical relationship between the artist’s control and the spontaneity of materials. In the act of creation, my role may be limited to guiding the materials, without being able to fully dictate their presentation. Each tea stain carries an element of randomness, shaped by natural factors such as time, environment, and humidity. This unpredictability brings the work closer to the spontaneous growth of nature, perhaps reflecting the vitality of the material and the inherent unpredictability of change.

Moreover, the tea stains on the paper are not just marks of interaction between the material and the surface but also evidence of a “consumption” process. As the tea leaves release their color and aroma, they gradually lose their original form, leaving behind imprints imbued with a sense of wabi-sabi (beauty in imperfection). This has made me reflect on whether an artwork can convey meaning through the consumption of materials. What happens to the image when the tea stains gradually fade or are further altered by the environment? This irreversible process of consumption might express the fragility of life and memory, allowing the audience to experience the inevitability of time’s passage.

It is this transience that gives the work its unique value and emotional power, reminding us that even the smallest traces carry meaning, no matter how fleeting they may be.


3. Using Food Bricks to Repair a Wall/Architecture

In the Summer Show, I explored the possibilities of food materials in architecture through a tea brick sculpture. Moving forward, I plan to use food bricks to repair a wall in a real environment, further combining the expressions of sculpture and performance art.

This attempt is not only a material experiment but also a reflection on the relationship between human-made objects and the natural environment. Food bricks naturally undergo gradual changes over time, giving the impression that their presence is both a process of repair and one of inevitable transformation. As the food bricks are subtly altered by natural influences, they remind us that architecture is not always permanent or unchanging—human-made structures can also be temporary and fragile.

Furthermore, if building materials were alive and capable of change, how would this reshape our understanding of architecture? Could these gradually transforming sculptures offer new perspectives on space, time, and the essence of materials? This experiment seeks to challenge conventional notions of permanence in architecture and provoke thought about the dynamic interactions between materials and their environment.
