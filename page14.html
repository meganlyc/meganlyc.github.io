Silent Dialogue

My work explores the boundaries between sculpture and painting, merging organic and industrial elements to engage the senses and question materiality. Using compressed tea bricks, reinforced by partially exposed steel, I create columns that stand in conversation with their surroundings, emphasizing both structure and fragility. The visible steel prompts viewers to reconsider tea as a construction material, challenging traditional views on durability and the role of organic materials in architecture.

These tea brick columns interact with the ground, where tea leaves trace shadows or lines, creating a play between two-dimensional and three-dimensional forms. This arrangement blurs the line between surface and volume, allowing the sculptures to function both as solid structures and as lines within the space, redefining the boundaries of the room. These "drawn" shadows echo the columns’ forms, creating a visual and spatial dialogue that prompts reflection on the interaction of flatness and depth, presence and absence.

The scent of tea permeates the exhibition, adding an invisible dimension that grounds the viewer’s experience in a multi-sensory realm. This olfactory element ties the ephemeral with the tangible, deepening the connection between the viewer and the organic material. Building on this sensory exploration, I developed a series of tea-based works that blur the boundaries between painting and drawing, with tea leaves themselves becoming the medium. Tea serves as “paint,” leaving distinctive marks that reveal its natural texture and color. These pieces extend the dialogue between sculpture and painting, inviting viewers to engage with the philosophical interplay between material, space, and sensory perception, while challenging conventional boundaries of art forms. Through these small tea-leaf geometries, I distill the relationships of boundary and space, presenting the essence of larger works. This approach encourages viewers to sense the complexity within simplicity, recognizing that space need not be filled with weighty material to be fully perceived.
