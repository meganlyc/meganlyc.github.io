In Unit 1 and Unit 2, I have been exploring the relationship between materials and forms, as well as how to reconstruct existing elements to create new forms. In Unit 1, I combined hand modeling with 3D printing to enlarge and transform existing shapes; in Unit 2, I built complex layers by stacking and combining existing objects. This led me to think: Is it possible to use a material with cultural significance and sculptural characteristics, one that can exist as an independent unit while also being reconstructed into new forms? This line of thinking brought me back to tea bricks—a material that carries my personal memories and cultural background. The weight, volume, and inherent sculptural quality of tea bricks make them an ideal medium for creation, allowing me to extend the logic of reconstruction and form exploration into new works

Working process

Material Selection

I seem to have a deep connection with tea. The place I live in has a rich tea culture. My father is an avid collector of tea, and my uncle is a tea merchant who owns a tea factory. When I first observed tea bricks, I noticed their weight, volume, and form, which seemed very suitable for sculpture. I began wondering if I could use this material to create something.

Out of curiosity about the process of making tea bricks, I contacted my uncle and went to Yunnan to learn how to compress tea leaves into tea bricks. I spent over a month making many tea bricks myself. During this continuous learning and practice, I not only reflected on the transformation of materials but also realized, to my surprise, that I was already creating a sculpture—tea bricks themselves are sculptures. This realization prompted me to question and imagine: If I use this “sculpture” to create another sculpture, what would it be.

From Triangle to Rectangle: The Evolution of Form and Reflection on Choices / Why the Rectangle?

In one of my previous works, I created an inverted triangular sculpture using tea bricks and placed it in a very confined space. The triangle itself is a geometric shape with strong directionality and sharpness. Combined with the heavy texture of the tea bricks, it created a sense of oppression and tension. This combination of form and space highlighted the instability and extreme presence of the triangle, evoking a physical sense of oppression and psychological tension in the viewer.
However, after completing this work, I began to reflect on the visual effects and spatial representation of the triangle. While it effectively conveyed a sense of tension and confrontation, I gradually realized that the closed and highly directional shape might be overly singular in its expression. I started to question: if the triangle were transformed into a more open, stable, and expansive form, could the work create greater possibilities within the space?


This reflection led me to choose the rectangle as the core form of my next sculpture. Compared to the triangle, the rectangle is more balanced and stable. Its linear characteristics make it easier to establish a sense of continuity and dialogue with the space. I designed the rectangle as a structure extending diagonally, aiming to break the closed nature of the triangle and emphasize a more open and dynamic spatial experience. With the rectangular form, the viewer’s gaze can traverse the space along the directionality of the work, no longer confined to a singular emotion but interacting more dynamically with the space through its expansiveness.


Moreover, the choice of the rectangle was also influenced by the scale and arrangement of the space. In the triangular work, the spatial constraints amplified the impact of the triangle. However, the rectangle required a larger space to fully showcase its extension. For the new work, I deliberately chose a more open environment, allowing the rectangle to breathe and interact more freely. This integration of form and space not only freed the work from a sense of confinement but also enabled it to blend more organically with its surroundings.
The shift from triangle to rectangle is not merely a change in form but also a rethinking of how the work engages with space. The rectangle provides an opportunity to explore a more stable yet dynamic language, while also broadening my understanding of the relationship between sculptural form and spatial interaction.

Making process

I began constructing the tea brick column by stacking and compressing the tea bricks layer by layer, using adhesive glue to securely bond each layer. Careful alignment was required for each layer to ensure the column’s stability and uniformity. Once the stacking was completed, the glue was left to air dry naturally, allowing the structure to solidify and gain long-lasting support.

Afterward, I sought assistance from my school’s metal workshop to create a metal frame precisely sized to provide the necessary support for the tea brick column. This frame not only added structural reinforcement but also ensured the sculpture’s overall stability. Once completed, the metal frame was carefully inserted into the interior of the tea brick column, fully embedded within it, further enhancing the sculpture’s durability and stability.

I recall having a critique session in the studio during the making process, where I received some feedback on the structure of the tea brick column. Some comments pointed out that the way the tea bricks were glued and assembled seemed somewhat unconventional (as bricks, shouldn’t they be stacked layer by layer?), while others mentioned that my arrangement looked more like decorative tiles. Another suggestion was to create a mold to form a single piece, which could then be joined on all four sides to form the sculpture.

I spent a significant amount of time reflecting on these questions. However, due to time constraints at the time, the limited quantity of materials I had, and considerations of feasibility, I decided to proceed with the current approach and see what would happen. In the end, I completed the column, reaching a height of over 2 meters.

Installing and Space

During the installation phase, I primarily considered the placement of the sculpture and its relationship with the surrounding space.

Before installation, I visited the site and noticed a wall column near the window. This immediately reminded me of my own sculpture, which is also column-shaped. I thought it might be interesting to create a dialogue between the sculpture and the wall column, adding a sense of connection between the two.

Inspired by my admiration for Richard Serra and his leaning works, I became curious about such forms and the states they convey. With this in mind, I decided to experiment with placing the tea brick column leaning on the windowsill to see how it would look and interact with the space.



Making of the Tea Leaves Strip

At the time, I had some leftover dried tea leaves that I wanted to make use of. While thinking about what I could create with them, it happened to be in the afternoon, and the sunlight inspired me with the concept of a “shadow.” I decided to try making a shadow-like copy of the “tea brick column” using the tea leaves.

Wanting to maintain a connection between the tea brick column and the wall column next to the windowsill, I ultimately used the tea leaves strip to link the two. This created a visual connection between them, allowing for a more cohesive relationship in the composition. 



 About Painting

After the exhibition, I began reflecting on the works displayed. They existed in the space as two lines, and this spatial relationship sparked new thoughts in my mind. I started wondering if these lines, arranged and interacting in three-dimensional space, could be simplified into a visual expression on a flat surface. This realization sparked my interest in the form and possibilities of lines, serving as the starting point for my painting practice.

The exhibition made me more aware of the relationship between material, form, and expression. In the exhibition, tea bricks and tea leaves, as the core materials of the sculptures, created a dialogue within three-dimensional space. In contrast, painting offered me the opportunity to return to the flat surface, focusing on the purity of the line itself. I chose to continue using tea leaves as my painting material, as they not only extended the core medium of the exhibition but also allowed me to reimagine the expressive potential of the material and the line in a two-dimensional space.

Through the creation of these paintings, I attempted to distill the line relationships present in the exhibition works, liberating them from the complexity of three-dimensional space and transforming them into rhythm and contrast within two-dimensional space. Compared to the sculptural works in the exhibition, painting is a more simplified language, yet it continues the core concepts of the exhibition. This process allowed me to revisit the insights gained from the exhibition while exploring a new possibility within flat-surface creation.
